**Purpose:** Final-push blueprint for the 4-day sprint and the first 90 days after.

## 1. Seven best-in-class design patterns MLC should steal

The visual moat is obvious once you look outside the memorial category: **no one in memorial is doing craft-editorial design, and no one selling physical memorial objects treats the digital profile as the primary product.** The closest visual cousin to what MLC should look like is not another memorial site — it is Aimé Leon Dore × NYT "Overlooked" × Shinola. Seven patterns to borrow, each tied to a source brand and an exact MLC application:

**The manufactured-in-place signature block.** Shinola locks up "Shinola Detroit" as a near-notarial mark, recurring on category pages, footers, and engraved into the physical product. MLC creates **"MLC · SANTA MARIA, CA · EST. 2026"** set in Staatliches on Charcoal, Chrome hairline above, Gold pinstripe below, appearing on every product card, every profile footer, and physically engraved on Heritage Books and plaques. Tagline: *"Made in Santa Maria. Carried forever."* This single pattern signals heritage commerce more than any trust badge can.

**Full-bleed human portrait with two-line Staatliches headline, zero body copy above the fold.** Aimé Leon Dore opens lookbooks with a full-bleed photograph of an actual person — not a model, but a real human connected to the brand — and lets two or three words of condensed display type carry the weight. MLC's hero becomes a full-bleed portrait of a Santa Maria elder, shot in front of a '64 Impala or on a cemetery path, with one line: **"SOME LEGACIES / DESERVE FOREVER."** No sub-copy above the fold, one small Chrome scroll arrow. The restraint tells visitors this is a cultural object, not a SaaS product.

**Silent scroll — weight-change hierarchy, no animation.** The Atavist and NYT Overlooked series build rhythm entirely through font weight jumps, size shifts, and white space. There are no parallax effects, no scroll-jacking, no Lottie animations. The reader moves because they choose to. MLC should kill every hover animation except the existing shimmer, let typography do 100% of the hierarchy work, and follow a strict vertical scale: 128px hero headline → 48px section kicker → 18px body → 14px caption. The whole site should feel printable and mailable.

**Editorial-grid product cards with caption-style descriptions.** Shinola's "10,000 Hours" series, ALD's seasonal lookbooks, and Filson's feature pages all treat products as magazine editorial, not Amazon listings. Heritage Book, Plaque, Pet Tag, and Contractor Bundle pages use a 12-column editorial grid: hero image spans 8 columns, description in Cormorant Garamond Italic spans 4. Price lives small, in Chrome, lower right. Sample caption: *"The Heritage Book. 160 pages, Smyth-sewn, debossed in gold. Unlocks one Legacy Profile — yours, forever."* This is the anti-Amazon card.

**Cultural specificity as shibboleth, not gatekeeping.** The Marathon Clothing never apologizes for Crenshaw — the intersection is on the apparel, the heel tabs read "The Marathon Continues," and outsiders are invited in by being let in on the references, not by having them explained. MLC should use Spanish and Caló interchangeably without italicizing (italicizing Spanish visually others it), use the lowrider pinstripe as a 1-pixel Gold-on-Charcoal decorative rule throughout, and call sections what they actually are called in the culture — *Descansa en paz*, not "Rest in peace"; *La Familia*, not "Family." Never translate in-line. **Confidence in the dialect is what invites outsiders in.**

**Pull quote as monument.** NYT Overlooked and The Atavist float a single pull quote over a color block in serif italic at several line-heights above body size — acting as the grave marker inside the text. Every MLC Legacy Profile reserves one pull-quote slot for the person's chosen words to live by, set in Cormorant Garamond Italic at ~56px, Brandywine #8B0000, centered, Gold hairlines above and below. Example: *"Trabaja duro. Ama más duro."* — Héctor V., 1948–2024. This is the digital epitaph; give it real estate.

**Archive-as-navigation.** NYT Overlooked treats every obituary as a row in an eternal ledger. Legacy.com squanders the same idea with search-first stock-rose design. MLC should build **The MLC Index**: a public, typeset roll of every profile ever created, styled like a newspaper obituary roster, sortable by region and decade. One entry per line, set in the body font: **VÁZQUEZ, OSVALDO "BEAR" ·** *Santa Maria ·* 1952–2026 *·* Carpenter, father, lowrider. **This is what Legacy.com should have been — and it is MLC's SEO and PR moat.** Competitors show template gardens; MLC shows a monument wall.

The pattern-level diagnostic of the category: Legacy.com has been critiqued (Slate, 2017) for "schlocky design templates in shades borrowed from scrubs and onesies." Ancestry and 23andMe lean genealogical-science blue. StoryCorps looks like a nonprofit, not a product. TributeArchive, Everloved, Forever.com all default to soft florals and pastel gradients. The entire category is visually surrenderable.

## 2. MySpace magic — what actually made profiles feel personal

MySpace worked because it treated a profile as **a bedroom wall, not a business card.** Every feature mapped to a deep identity-signaling or belonging instinct. Peer-reviewed work on the Top 8 (De Scioli et al., 2011, *Evolutionary Psychology*, analyzing 10M+ friendship decisions) found that rank was best predicted by the alliance hypothesis — humans rank allies by perceived exclusivity of the bond — and danah boyd's 2006 "Friends, Friendsters, and Top 8" documented it as literal public social warfare. Profile songs worked as emotional priming: music is the most efficient identity marker humans possess, and one song set the mood before a single word of bio was read. Custom HTML/CSS worked as expression through limitation-breaking — the platform didn't want you editing code; you did anyway, and that transgression earned ownership. What failed was the chaos: autoplay across four tabs, unreadable black-on-purple text, malware injections, comment-wall glitter spam, and profiles that took 30 seconds to load.

**The translation principle for MLC: fixed structure, infinite content variability.** Give every profile the same seven blocks, each with disciplined format but completely open content. That is how you rebuild the MySpace emotional resonance without rebuilding the MySpace mess.

**The seven required components on every MLC Legacy Profile:**

1. **Hero Identity Card** — Full-bleed portrait, name, dates (1952–2024 for memorial; "Est. 1988, Santa Maria" for living; founding year + trade for business), one-line Signature Quote in a handwritten-style display font (Caveat Brush or Homemade Apple — never Comic Sans).

2. **Legacy Song** — One song, non-autoplay, big play button. Hard cap at one. Memorial = their favorite song picked by family. Living = current anthem. Business = shop theme. The single-song constraint is the discipline MySpace never had.

3. **Signature Block** — The emotional heart. A fixed-width "letter" module on cream background, Courier Prime typewriter font, left-aligned, reading like a handwritten note. Memorial variant = a family-written eulogy, max 400 words. Living variant = a first-person intro letter. Rich text allows only bold, italic, and line break — no emoji vomit. Ends with an actual signature: typed in the handwritten display font, or an uploaded image of the person's real signature. **The signature of the deceased, uploaded as an image, is sacred.**

4. **Top Connections (Top 8 done right)** — A grid of 6–8 profile cards, each with photo, name, and a **relationship label the owner writes themselves** ("my dad," "co-founder," "godmother," "the homie since kindergarten"). Role-based labels defuse the ranking drama — they are not ranked, they are named. This is the single most important MySpace-to-MLC translation.

5. **Timeline / Milestone Ribbon** — A vertical scrollable ribbon of 5–15 milestones: one photo + one date + one sentence each. Replaces the unstructured photo dump that turns Facebook timelines into noise.

6. **Gallery with Captions** — Up to 24 photos, **no caption means no post.** The caption requirement forces narrative control, which is how you transmit a life to a grandchild in 2055.

7. **Guest Book / Wall** — Memorial variant is the Guest Book with "light a candle" as the ceremonial like. Living variant is the Wall. Either way, **the steward moderates every post before publication.** That single design decision prevents MySpace-era chaos.

Optional eighth block for Heritage Book owners: a shelf showing which physical artifact unlocked the profile, with a photo of the object. Proof-of-ownership flex and retail flywheel.

What is deliberately missing: no custom HTML, no layout choice, no autoplay, no unlimited fonts, no glitter. **Your story, our frame.** That is the MLC promise.

## 3. The ADHD-friendly design system MLC should adopt

Synthesis drawn from WCAG 2.2 (now ISO/IEC 40500:2025), the W3C COGA Task Force "Making Content Usable" supplemental guidance, Nielsen Norman Group scanning research, WebAIM, Health.gov's Health Literacy Online, USWDS typography tokens, and ADHD-specific research from ADDitude, Understood.org, and the Verndale dyslexia/ADHD design study.

**The principle:** ADHD-friendly is older-adult-friendly is mobile-friendly is conversion-friendly. Every rule below helps MLC's 50+ Heritage Book buyers and ADHD-affected users simultaneously.

**Typography rules.** Body text sits at 18px mobile and 20px desktop — Health.gov recommends 19px for older adults; USWDS requires a 16px floor. Line-height is 1.5–1.6 for body, never tighter. Line length caps at 55–75 characters (Verndale caps at 70 for ADHD; sweet spot is ~65ch). Paragraphs maximum 3–4 lines, so attention can re-enter. Sans-serif for body on-screen — serif is acceptable only for long-form Heritage Book interiors. Stripe docs is the model: 18px, ~65ch, 1.6 line-height, three-line paragraphs.

**Color and contrast.** WCAG 2.2 AA minimum is 4.5:1 for body. But for ADHD and 50+ readers, avoid pure #000 on #FFF because halation ghosts the letterforms — use near-black (MLC's #1A1A1A) on warm off-white (#FAFAF7), not Chrome pure white. Use alternating section backgrounds (ivory → warm gray → ivory) to chunk content externally. Cap the palette at five colors total: 1 primary, 1 accent, 2 neutrals, 1 alert. MLC is already close — Charcoal, Brandywine, Chrome, Gold, Warm Gold — but should retire the Warm Gold as decorative and treat it as the single accent for pinstripes only.

**Whitespace and cognitive load.** 64–96px vertical padding between sections on desktop, 40–64px on mobile. CTAs get a 24px "halo" of empty space with nothing competing within 120px. **One primary CTA per section, one dominant CTA per screen** (Hick's Law, COGA "reduce branching"). Progressive disclosure for everything tertiary — collapsible FAQs, "read more" for deep cuts. Chunk every list and feature group to 3–5 items; anything more collapses. Apple product pages are the master reference for vertical breathing room.

**Scannable structure.** Eyebrow labels (small-caps Staatliches kickers above H2s) give ADHD scanners an anchor. Descriptive H2s, never cute — they must answer "what is this section?" in under two seconds. Pull quotes every 400–600 words in long-form act as attention re-entry points. Numbered steps externalize working memory (W3C COGA explicit pattern). Bold key terms, never full sentences. Linear's changelog is the template: eyebrow → H2 → two-line summary → pull-quote rhythm.

**Motion and actions.** Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` — non-negotiable (WCAG 2.3.3). No autoplay video, no auto-carousels — both are cited by COGA as attention-stealing. Hover interactions stay subtle and under 200ms. Button hierarchy: exactly one primary (filled) + one secondary (outline) per screen, tertiary is text link only. Touch targets 44×44px minimum. Button labels are verbs + objects: "Start Your Heritage Book," never "Learn More."

**The 14-rule MLC ADHD Checklist (apply across all 42 pages):**

1. Body 18px mobile / 20px desktop, line-height 1.55, max 65ch.
2. Paragraphs ≤4 lines. Descriptive H2s. Eyebrow labels above every major section.
3. Sans-serif for all UX body. Serif only for Heritage Book interior long-form.
4. Contrast ≥4.5:1 via #1A1A1A on #FAFAF7, never #000/#FFF.
5. Palette capped at 5 colors. Alternate two section backgrounds for chunking.
6. One primary CTA per section. One dominant CTA per screen.
7. Chunk lists to 3–5 items; collapse the rest.
8. 64–96px desktop / 40–64px mobile vertical rhythm. 24px CTA halo.
9. Alternate text / image / color block — never more than two text blocks in a row.
10. Pull quote every 400–600 words in long-form.
11. Bold key terms only; italics sparingly (18% harder to read).
12. `prefers-reduced-motion` honored. No autoplay. Hover ≤200ms.
13. Touch targets ≥44×44px. Focus indicators always visible (WCAG 2.2 SC 2.4.11).
14. Consistent help location, autosave forms, no cognitive auth tests.

Publish this as the **/design-system page** with a line of copy no competitor can say honestly: *"MLC is the first legacy platform explicitly designed for ADHD brains. Short sentences. Bold key words. Steps, not paragraphs. Because your family's story shouldn't take 20 minutes to read."* This is differentiation and accessibility simultaneously. Ship it.

The category's lesson comes from Peloton, Apple, Nintendo amiibo, SFMOMA QR codes, and Penfolds wine identification: **the best physical-to-digital bridges show both objects in one frame, use three-word causal-chain copy, and treat the QR code as a typographic character rather than a utility sticker.** Nintendo's amiibo copy is the cleanest version — "Tap an amiibo to your game system's NFC reader" — one physical action, one digital result, figure on the left, game screen on the right, ripple in between. Apple's HIG is explicit about Apple Watch: "designed to blur the boundaries between physical object and software." Peloton leads with a photograph of the bike's touchscreen showing a live class, so the digital content visibly lives inside the physical product. A warning buried in the LEGO Hidden Side post-mortem: the physical object must stay valuable even if the digital layer disappears. Vinyl records survive because the disc plays without the download code.

**What works without testimonials (research-backed):** founder story with face + name + personal stakes (Warby Parker, Everlane, Forever.com — sites with founder video show ~80% longer engagement, Vibrantsnap aggregate); specific mission that names an enemy, not boilerplate; transparent pricing copy ("$79 once. ## 6. Fraunces vs Lato — the definitive verdict

**Use Lato as MLC's single web body font. Drop Fraunces. Drop Big Shoulders. Final stack: Staatliches + Lato + Cormorant Garamond Italic. Three fonts. Ship it.**

**The 50+ demographic wins for Lato unambiguously.** Every source on older-adult screen readability converges: humanist sans-serifs with high x-height, open apertures, and unambiguous letterforms (Frutiger, Segoe UI, Verdana, and Lato) outperform serifs on backlit displays. Health.gov's Health Literacy Online explicitly names Lato as a recommended familiar sans-serif. Older eyes face reduced contrast sensitivity, glare sensitivity, and focusing difficulty. Fraunces's thin strokes and soft terminals become fragile below 18px — Undercase Type's own documentation describes it as "a bit critical and almost too delicate" below 14px. On a 50+ iPhone at 16–18px, Lato stays stable; Fraunces flickers.

**iPhone rendering wins for Lato.** Lato was drawn by Łukasz Dziedzic specifically for screen rendering; its generous x-height and wide apertures survive sub-pixel rendering. Fraunces has an optical-size axis — genuinely impressive — but even optically sized, its high stroke contrast and quirky terminals demand more of the eye on a crowded product page.

**The stack pairing wins for Lato.** Your current stack has **three high-personality fonts already**: Staatliches (condensed display), Big Shoulders (industrial condensed sans), and Cormorant Garamond Italic. Adding Fraunces as a fourth voice creates four competing personalities and violates the ADHD cognitive-load principles above. Lato is deliberately neutral — humanist-warm without personality noise. It lets Staatliches do the dramatic heralding, lets Cormorant Italic carry the literary intimacy, and lets the copy itself do the meaning. **Contrast, not crowding, is what makes a stack feel intentional.**

**Tone — the harder call, but still Lato.** Fraunces is tempting for memorial + heritage — warm, literary, Mailchimp-adjacent. But the serif emotional register in MLC's stack is **already handled by Cormorant Garamond Italic pull quotes**. Memorial tone doesn't mean every pixel must feel like a eulogy. It means the emotional beats land — which they do via Staatliches hero moments and Cormorant pull quotes. Lato is the calm paper the emotion is printed on.

**Drop Big Shoulders.** Staatliches and Big Shoulders are both condensed impactful display sans-serifs doing similar jobs — they are too close in DNA, creating muddy hierarchy. Use Staatliches for hero, eyebrows, and kickers (ceremonial uppercase). Use **Lato Black (900) or Lato Bold (700) for headlines.** The contrast between tall/narrow/uppercase Staatliches and wide/humanist/sentence-case Lato Bold is stronger and more legible than Staatliches vs Big Shoulders.

**Final three-font MLC stack:**

- **Staatliches** — hero display, eyebrow labels, section kickers, ceremonial all-caps
- **Lato** — headlines (Black/Bold), body (Regular 18–20px), UI, nav, forms, all product copy
- **Cormorant Garamond Italic** — pull quotes and epitaph moments only

For Heritage Book print interiors only, consider **Literata** (optimized by Google Books for long-form print reading). That decision lives off the website.

## 7. Lean harder into 805 / Chicano / Lowrider

**Thesis: specificity is the moat, not a ceiling.** In culture-forward categories, brands that stay specific build cultural gravity; brands that sand off their origins to "appeal to everyone" die the slow death of the commodity. MLC is not CRM software — it is a cultural artifact masquerading as software, and it must behave accordingly.

The evidence is uniform. **The Marathon Clothing** stayed hyper-local to a single Crenshaw and Slauson intersection and went from one smart-store on a disinvested block to a national icon with Puma collaborations precisely because it refused to generalize. **Supreme** stayed downtown NYC, stayed drop-based, and became a $2.1B acquisition. **Aimé Leon Dore** put Queens in every lookbook and opened London and Tokyo without losing the Queens stoop. **Carhartt WIP** kept actual working-class DNA and became the most-cited non-luxury streetwear brand of the 2010s. **Patagonia** doubled down on environmentalism, sued the government, donated the company, and the market grew. **Daily Paper** kept African-diaspora identity front and center and landed in SoHo and Selfridges. The graveyard tells the inverse story: **Abercrombie** collapsed when specificity-as-exclusion stopped working (the failure mode matters — MLC's specificity celebrates, which is categorically different); **Gap's 2010 rebrand** from the iconic blue box to generic Helvetica reverted in six days after universal backlash; **Banana Republic** started as a safari-adventure outfitter and spent 15 years in the wilderness after throwing its DNA out for mall-tier basics.

**Where MLC leans harder (specificity is the conversion lever):**

- **Homepage hero.** Lowrider hydraulic three-wheel silhouette against a Santa Maria sunset, not a stock family. **The 805 must appear above the fold.** Copy: *"Your people. Your rides. Your gente. Forever on the 805."*
- **Heritage Book page.** Book in a real hand (Anthony's, ideally). Pinstripe detail, embossed leather finish on camera. *"Heritage Books are how we keep our people. Every profile starts with one."*
- **About / Founder page.** Full-throat Santa Maria origin story. Car club lineage, why $79-forever vs subscription. First-person, Spanglish welcome, no corporate voice.
- **Specific Legacy Drops** (Año Nuevo, Día de los Muertos, Cinco de Mayo, Pachuco memorial drops). Full cultural language, Spanish and English side by side, never italicized, never translated inline.
- **Anthony Raygoza Sr. Legacy Drop.** Gold-standard template every new user clones emotionally.

**Where MLC stays culturally neutral (these pages serve function, not identity):**

- **Checkout.** Stripe-clean. Trust, clarity, speed. Culture sold them — get out of the way.
- **Pet tags.** Pets are universal. Chihuahua or golden retriever — warm MLC-branded but no lowrider language.
- **Baby Legacy.** Tender, universal, gender-neutral. Parents outside the 805 need to imagine their newborn here.
- **Contractor / small-business bundles.** B2B copy. Outcomes, not origin. "Keep your crew's story alive" is the furthest you flex.
- **Help Center, T&Cs, privacy, account settings.** Boring on purpose.

**Copy patterns that invite outsiders without diluting:** Do not italicize Spanish — italicizing it marks it as foreign in an English product, which it isn't. Do not translate inline — "My tío built this car in 1974" reads cleanly from context; parentheticals code as tourist. Open outsider-facing pages with universal emotional truth (grief, legacy, love, family) and drop into specifics once they're in — the universal hooks, the specific holds. Let photography do the cultural work; flake paint, plaques, primer'd Monte Carlos, Saint Jude medallions communicate faster than adjectives. Credit real people by name — car clubs, pinstripers, upholsterers, binders. Names are specificity, SEO, and proof simultaneously.

**The no-go risks:** performative Spanglish ("vato," "ese" sprinkled for flavor in UI copy is cosplay, not voice); cliché iconography (skulls and marigolds year-round cheapen Día de los Muertos — reserve seasonal iconography for seasons); over-explaining ("What is a lowrider?" glossary signals "this isn't really for you" — respect readers' ability to Google); stock imagery (one Shutterstock lowrider and the whole brand loses credibility — every image must be original, shot in Santa Maria, Oxnard, Lompoc, Watsonville); celebrity chasing (don't buy a co-sign before the product is earned — community first, then celebrity, the Nipsey sequence).

**Bottom line:** Specificity is the moat. The 805/Chicano/lowrider identity doesn't shrink MLC's market — it creates the only market where $79-forever Legacy Profiles make cultural sense. Neutral memorial tech already exists. MLC wins by being the only place that feels like home to a specific people first, and invites everyone else in as guests. Guests respect the house. Residents buy Heritage Books.

## 8. Single homepage CTA — free-first, waitlist-focused

**Go free-first. Single dominant CTA: `Claim Your Free Legacy Profile →`. Secondary text-link: `See the Heritage Book ($199)`. Ship it.**

Research converges unambiguously. WordStream reports emails with one CTA drive 371% more clicks and 1,617% more sales than multi-CTA; WiserNotify aggregate shows single-CTA landing pages can lift CVR up to 266%; Hick's Law and Nielsen Norman converge on fewer choices = faster decisions. Homepages are a partial exception — they function as an index — but analyses from Act-On and Telerik both find high-performing homepages carry one dominant CTA plus one or two subordinate CTAs above fold, not a grid. More than four CTAs above fold measurably damages primary conversion.

**Free-first beats paid-first for unknown brands.** CXL funnel research and Basecamp's famous HighRise test (free-trial vs "see plans and pricing") both show low-friction entry dramatically expands the top of funnel. Kahneman loss-aversion says first-time visitors will not part with $199 for a brand they met forty seconds ago. **A paid-first hero on a zero-testimonial Day-1 site is asking cold traffic for a $199 decision on a stranger's word — the hardest possible ask in e-commerce.**

Multi-product D2C pattern confirms it: Apple rotates a single hero (one product at a time), Peloton uses one "Shop Bikes" CTA with everything else subordinate, Allbirds and Warby Parker run a single binary ("Shop Men / Shop Women") plus hero product, Glossier runs a single hero product + "Shop Now" with mission on /about, and ALD runs pure editorial — but only because it's a mature brand with a cult audience. **None of them show six products as equal tiles above fold. You pick a hero for the month, not a menu.**

**The exact Day-1 homepage CTA structure:**

**Hero headline:** *"Your name doesn't end. Your story doesn't disappear."*

**Subhead (value prop above primary CTA):** *"A forever home for your family, your ranfla, and your gente — built by one guy in Santa Maria, not a $15/month subscription."*

**Primary CTA (large, Brandywine fill, centered):** **`Claim Your Free Legacy Profile →`**
Microcopy: *"Free forever. Takes 3 minutes. No credit card."*

**Secondary CTA (outlined, smaller, below primary):** **`See the Heritage Book ($199)`**
Microcopy: *"Our flagship. 160-page, hardcover, one-time purchase."*

**Mobile sticky CTA: yes.** Shopify and Baymard 2024 show sticky bottom CTAs lift mobile CVR 15–30% on long scrolls. Non-negotiable. Bottom bar on mobile: `Claim Your Free Profile` button + `Shop →` text-link.

Why "Claim" beats "Sign up" — ownership language works better in legacy and cultural contexts (Mailmodo and PartnerStack CTA tests). Why free-first breaks the Linktree pricing argument — Linktree is $180/year recurring; MLC's "free forever + $79 once for premium" literally breaks their math in one line. The Heritage Book's hero moment lives in email #3 of the welcome sequence, not on the homepage.
