The Music

Celtic bagpipes, Mexico City mariachi, and the corrido of the crossing

+

Most people don't know this: Galicia is Celtic. The gaita gallega — the Galician bagpipe — sounds like Ireland because the ancient peoples were connected. Your Vázquez name comes from a place where Celtic music never died. Then that line moved to Mexico City, where the corrido became the working-class newspaper — the music that documented everything the system refused to record. Two sounds. Both in your blood.

The Food

From Galician granite coast to Mexico City kitchens — taste where they lived

+

Food is the one thing that crosses every border intact. Put on Week 1's music. Cook one of these dishes. The combination connects something that reading can't reach. Start with the pozole — it's the most Mexico City dish on this list, and it's the easiest to make on a Sunday.

Cook This Week

🍽️

Pozole Rojo — Mexico City's Celebration Dish

In Mexico City, pozole was the dish for anything that mattered — baptisms, quinceañeras, homecomings. Hominy corn, pork, dried chiles, topped with cabbage and radish at the table. Grandpa Joe knew this dish. Make it on a Sunday with the mariachi playing.

🍽️

Tamales — The Food of Crossings

Every family that made the journey from Mexico to California brought tamale knowledge with them. The masa, the filling, the corn husks — it all traveled north. If you have a family tamale recipe, make those. That recipe is worth more than any document in these archives.

Pulpo a la Gallega — The Dish of Galicia

Octopus served on wooden plates with olive oil, paprika, and salt. That's it. This dish has been eaten at every fair, every feast day, every family gathering in Galicia for 700 years. Your Vázquez ancestors — the ones who never left Spain — ate this. Make it once just to say you did.

Tarta de Santiago — Galician Almond Cake

Almond flour, eggs, citrus. No wheat, no dairy. Baked in Galicia since the medieval pilgrim era. The cross of Saint James pressed into powdered sugar on top. Simple, ancient, and surprisingly easy to make. This is the dessert of your family's oldest homeland.

The Archives

Find them by name — your family's actual documents exist in these databases

+

This week you stop reading about your family and start finding them. Birth records. Baptism cards. Border crossing documents. Census pages with their handwriting. Start with FamilySearch — it's free and it's where most people find their first real document. Then go deeper.

Search These — In Order

1. FamilySearch — Mexico City Catholic Church Records, 1514–1970

The most likely place to find Grandpa Joe's baptism or his parents' marriage record. Free. No account required to search.

Search: "Vázquez" → Mexico City / Distrito Federal → year range 1890–1930 Open this collection →

2. PARES — Spanish National Archive (Hidalgo Nobility)

Spain's free national archive. Contains the Padrón de Hidalgos — the nobility registry from the Real Chancillería de Valladolid. Your family's Galician hidalgo status from 1547 is documented in collections like this.

Search: "Vázquez" → Galicia → Real Chancillería de Valladolid collection Open PARES →

3. NARA — US Border Crossing Records, 1895–1960

The physical card your grandfather filled out when he crossed into California — his handwriting, his described appearance, his stated occupation. This document exists somewhere in here.

Search: "Vázquez" or "Vasquez" → El Paso or Nogales → 1920–1945. Your library may have free Ancestry access. Search on Ancestry →

4. Bracero Archive — UC Davis

Photos, contracts, oral histories from the Bracero Program (1942–1964). Even if your grandfather wasn't in the program — look at the Santa Barbara / Santa Maria photos. These faces are your grandfather's neighbors. Some may be people he knew.

Browse by region: Santa Barbara / Santa Maria. Look at the photo collections first. Open archive →

5. AGN — Archivo General de la Nación (Mexico City)

Mexico's national archive. Land records, colonial padrones, notarial records. The oldest and deepest archive on this list. Takes patience — but this is where the records from 1600s and 1700s Mexico City live.

Start: "Ramo Padrones" → Vázquez → Distrito Federal. Use the accent mark in your search. Open AGN →

The Music

Norteño, conjunto, and the Tejano sound that came out of South Texas

+

The García family line runs through Nuevo León, Mexico → Sinton, Texas → California. Nuevo León is norteño country — the accordion, the bajo sexto, the corrido. When your family crossed into Texas, that sound mixed with Czech and German polka traditions in the borderlands and became conjunto. Then it became Tejano. Los Alegres de Terán are from General Terán, Nuevo León — the exact region your García/Montemayor line comes from. That's not a coincidence. That's your music.

From General Terán, Nuevo León — the same region your Montemayor/García line traces to. They defined norteño music for decades. Two voices, one accordion, one bajo sexto. This is what played at dances in the towns your great-great-grandmother Clara grew up near. Start with Tragos Amargos or Carta Jugada.

The Food

Tex-Mex, norteño home cooking, and what 18 people ate around one table

+

Clara and Francisco García raised 18 children in Sinton, Texas through the Depression. Think about what that kitchen looked like. What the food had to be — filling, affordable, made from what the land and the market gave you. These dishes are from that world. Make them on a Sunday. Feed a crowd.

Cook This Week

🍽️

Caldo de Res — South Texas Beef Soup

The Sunday soup of the borderlands. Beef shanks, corn, squash, carrots, cabbage. Inexpensive, filling, made in a big pot for a big family. This is exactly what Clara García would have made for 18 kids in the 1940s. Serve with warm flour tortillas and don't skimp on the broth.

🍽️

Breakfast Tacos — Texas Morning Staple

Flour tortillas, scrambled eggs, potato, chorizo, cheese. This is the Texas Mexican-American breakfast that separates people who grew up in South Texas from everyone else. Not a corn tortilla. Flour. Big and soft. This is what your grandmother Elvira ate growing up as the youngest of 18.

Find on Food52 →
🍽️

Pan de Campo — Texas Cowboy Bread

Cast iron skillet bread from the ranch and farm tradition of South Texas. Simple flour, baking powder, lard, salt, water. Baked in a Dutch oven over coals or in the oven. The bread that fed the people who fed the country — farmworkers, ranch hands, the García family. Make it in cast iron.

Find on Food52 →
🍽️

Menudo — Sunday Morning Ritual

Every South Texas family has a menudo story. It's the soup that appears after celebrations, after late nights, on Sunday mornings when the whole family comes together. Tripe, hominy, red chile broth, lime, oregano. It's an acquired taste. It's also a rite of passage. Your family made it.

The Archives

Find Faustino, Clara, Elvira — they're in here by name

+

Your García/Montemayor family has confirmed documented records — the 1940 US Census showing Francisco García and family in Sinton, Texas. Faustino Montemayor's death certificate from 1950. These documents exist in public archives right now. This week you find them — and then you keep going.

Search These — In Order

1. FamilySearch — 1940 US Census, San Patricio County, Texas

The 1940 Census captured Francisco García's household in Sinton — all 18 children, 60 hours/week farming. This document exists. You can find it free on FamilySearch right now.

Search: "Francisco Garcia" → San Patricio County, Texas → 1940 Census collection Open 1940 Census →

2. FindAGrave — Faustino Montemayor (1852–1950)

Your great-great-grandfather lived to 98. His grave is in Sinton, Texas — likely Eternal Rest Cemetery or a local Catholic cemetery. His grave record and possibly a photo are on FindAGrave right now.

Search: "Faustino Montemayor" → San Patricio County, Texas. Also search "María Montemayor" for other family members. Search FindAGrave →

3. FamilySearch — Texas Death Certificates, 1903–1982

Faustino Montemayor's death certificate from 1950 is in this collection. Death certificates often list parents' names, birthplace, and cause of death — giving you one more generation back into Nuevo León, Mexico.

Search: "Faustino Montemayor" → Texas → 1950. Also try "Montemayor" broadly for other family records. Open Texas Death Records →

4. FamilySearch — Nuevo León, Mexico Civil Registration

If Faustino's death certificate lists his birthplace in Nuevo León, this is your next stop. Civil registration records go back to the 1860s and cover births, marriages, and deaths across Nuevo León municipalities including General Terán and surrounding areas.

Search: "Montemayor" → Nuevo León → filter by municipality. Look for Faustino born approximately 1850–1855. Open Nuevo León Records →

5. Chronicling America — Sinton, Texas Newspapers (1900–1950)

The Library of Congress's free newspaper archive. Local South Texas papers sometimes published birth announcements, obituaries, and community news. Search "Garcia" or "Montemayor" + Sinton to see if your family made the local paper — births, weddings, deaths, community events.

Search: "Garcia" + "Sinton" or "San Patricio." Filter by Texas newspapers, date range 1920–1955. Search Chronicling America →
